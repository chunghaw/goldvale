What actually monetizes in pet apps

✅ Categories that print money

  • Dog training — Woofz ~$20M ARR (2025, bootstrapped, profitable; 21M downloads, ~120K subs ≈ 0.6% conversion); Dogo 10M+ users (~$10/mo); GoodPup acquired by Rover. → Tideline's lane.
  • Daily health-habit AI — Cal AI ~$40M TTM (calorie-from-photo), bootstrapped, acquired by MyFitnessPal. The sticky-daily-loop playbook. → Oscar / Pawmanac.
  • Recurring pet-safety — Tractive €100M+ ARR (GPS hardware + subscription). People DO pay recurring for pet safety — but with hardware.

⚠️ Monetization traps

  • Lost-pet search is largely free/nonprofit — Petco Love Lost & Finding Rover give facial-recognition away; PawBoost ≈ $1M cumulative. → Reunipet's ceiling.
  • Health records monetize B2B, not B2C — PetDesk $20M ARR from clinics; 11pets weak consumer. → Oscar must be a companion, not a diary.
  • Breed/DNA & foresight is episodic — Embark ~$48M is hardware one-off; the need is consulted rarely. → Pawmanac's weak loop.
  • Big MAU + weak loop = weak money — the AI-pet app "Pengu" had the most users but the least revenue. Retention > downloads, always.

Reliability varies: Woofz/PetDesk are founder-stated; Cal AI/Embark are widely-reported estimates; Tractive is a confirmed milestone; lost-pet "free" status is confirmed. Treat ranges as reliable, point estimates as rough.

Reunipet
Your lost pet is probably already in a shelter's database. We find them by description, not just by face.
Craft #1
Tech/DB 5 Design 5 Impact 4 Orig. 5 $ Realism 2
Who it's for

Frantic pet owners in the first 72 hours after a dog or cat goes missing — especially people whose pet just entered a different county's shelter system where Petco Love Lost has no integrated feed. Secondary: good-samaritan finders who photographed a stray and want to route it to the right owner without driving it anywhere.

The pitch

When a pet goes missing, the cruel irony is that it's often already safe — sitting in a municipal shelter 20 miles away whose intake feed the owner has no idea exists. Petco Love Lost and Finding Rover try to solve this with FACE recognition, but they only see pets inside their own partner network (ShelterLuv/ShelterBuddy shelters that opted in), and face-matching collapses on black cats, huskies, side-profile phone photos, and any pet whose finder only typed a text description instead of uploading a clean photo. Reunipet attacks the problem from the data side, not the biometric side. We ingest open municipal shelter intake feeds (Socrata SODA portals from Austin, Sonoma, Dallas, Bloomington, Long Beach and more), open adoptable-pet APIs (RescueGroups, Petfinder), and community sighting reports into one Aurora PostgreSQL graph. Then we match on THREE things at once: a CLIP image embedding of the pet photo, a SEMANTIC text embedding of the description ("brindle pittie, torn left ear, blue collar, answers to Mango"), and a geo/time corridor (how far could this animal plausibly have traveled since it was lost?). A Bedrock Claude agent reads every candidate row — even the ugly ones with no photo and a one-line "found brown dog near 5th & Main" intake note — reasons about whether it's the same pet across all three signals, and writes the owner a plain-English ranked brief: "92% — this stray dog booked into Travis County AC on May 28 matches your Mango on coat, ear notch, and location; here's the kennel number and the shelter's hold-expiry date. Call them today." It's a data-coverage and reasoning product, not a face-scanner.

End-to-end user experience
  1. Report in 60 seconds — photo OR words, either works
    Owner lands on the web app (Vercel), taps 'My pet is missing.' They drop one phone photo and/or just type free text: 'Mango, neutered male brindle pit-bull mix, ~50 lbs, torn left ear tip, faded blue collar, microchip yes, last seen near Zilker Park Austin around 6pm May 27.' Pin the last-seen spot on a map. No account wall — they can do this before signing up. Outcome: a structured lost-pet record is created and immediately embedded (CLIP image vector + text vector).
  2. Instant first sweep across open data, not just our own users
    On submit, Reunipet runs a pgvector similarity search against everything already ingested in the last 21 days: municipal shelter intakes, found-pet community posts, and adoptable listings within a geo/time corridor (radius widens with elapsed time). Within seconds the owner sees a ranked candidate wall — each card shows the source ('Travis County Animal Center intake, May 28'), a match score, and WHY it matched (coat, ear, distance). Outcome: owner sees real shelter rows they never knew to check, in the first session, with zero manual portal-hopping.
  3. The Claude reasoning brief on the top candidate
    Owner taps the #1 card. The Bedrock Claude agent has already read both records (their description + the shelter's terse intake note, possibly photoless) and writes a side-by-side reasoning brief: which features agree ('torn left ear' vs intake 'L ear notched'), which conflict ('intake lists female — verify, intake sex is frequently mis-keyed'), the travel plausibility, and a clear NEXT ACTION with the shelter's phone number, kennel ID, and computed hold-expiry countdown. A 'non-clinical, verify in person' disclaimer is shown. Outcome: owner gets a confident, cited, do-this-now recommendation instead of a raw probability.
  4. Set the standing watch (this is the retention hook)
    Owner creates a free account to 'keep watching.' Reunipet now stores their pet's vectors and re-runs the match continuously as NEW shelter intakes and sightings arrive (Aurora scheduled job + change-data trigger). They choose alert channels (email/SMS). Outcome: even if today's sweep is empty, they're covered the moment their pet is booked into ANY ingested feed — the most common real-world reunion path.
  5. A finder closes the loop from the other side
    Separately, a good samaritan who found a wandering cat opens Reunipet, taps 'I found a pet,' snaps a photo and types '2 white socks, gray tabby, very friendly, near Mueller.' This becomes a community-sighting record with its own vectors. Reunipet immediately checks it against all OPEN lost reports and pings any owner whose pet scores high. Outcome: two strangers get matched without the finder having to transport the animal or guess which of 6 apps the owner uses.
  6. Confirm and reunite — labeled outcome feeds the model
    When the owner confirms 'This is my pet!', they mark the match resolved and (optionally) how (shelter pickup / neighbor / etc.). That confirmed pair becomes a labeled positive example. Outcome: a real reunion, plus a ground-truth label that sharpens future scoring and powers the public 'reunions this week' analytics.
  7. Stay-found upgrade prompt
    Post-reunion, the app offers the freemium upgrade: a printable QR 'if found, scan me' tag and a permanent pet profile so a future finder reaches them instantly, plus 'priority watch' if it ever happens again. Outcome: a natural, non-predatory paywall placed at the moment of maximum gratitude, not maximum panic.
Why people pay (selling points)
  • Coverage over biometrics: we ingest OPEN municipal shelter feeds (Socrata) + RescueGroups + Petfinder + community sightings, so we surface pets sitting in shelters that Petco Love Lost's closed ShelterLuv/ShelterBuddy partner network never shows the owner.
  • Semantic description match, not just face match: type 'brindle pittie torn left ear' and we match shelter intakes that have NO photo at all — exactly the cases where Finding Rover's 138-facial-landmark model and Petco's 512-point face match return nothing.
  • Tri-signal scoring: CLIP image vector + description text vector + geo/time travel corridor are fused per candidate, so black cats and huskies (known face-match failure cases) still rank correctly on coat/markings/location.
  • A reasoning brief, not a probability bar: Bedrock Claude reads each candidate's messy intake note and tells the owner exactly which features agree, what to verify, the kennel ID, and the hold-expiry countdown — turning data into a do-this-today action.
  • Standing watch on new intakes: continuous re-matching as fresh shelter rows arrive means the most common reunion path (pet booked into a shelter days later) actually fires an alert.
  • Two-sided and frictionless: finders route a found animal by photo OR text without transporting it, and there's no account wall to file the initial report during the panic moment.
How it's different

Petco Love Lost: free and national, BUT its data is a CLOSED partner ecosystem — shelters must run ShelterLuv/ShelterBuddy and explicitly turn on the Petco Love API; matching is photo/face-only (up to 512 data points). It misses non-partner municipal shelters and any photoless or text-only found report. Reunipet differs by pulling OPEN municipal/Socrata intake feeds + RescueGroups/Petfinder that aren't in Petco's network, and by matching on semantic descriptions and geo/time, not faces. Finding Rover: 138-facial-landmark recognition from Univ. of Utah research — explicitly weak on blurry/side-profile/low-light photos, look-alike breeds, and entirely dependent on photo presence; Reunipet's text+image+geo fusion degrades gracefully when the photo is bad or absent. PawBoost / Pet FBI / Sniff & Found: these are broadcast-alert and crowdsourced-database plays ('AMBER alert for pets') — they amplify YOUR post to neighbors and social feeds but do little structured cross-matching against official shelter intake data; Reunipet is a matching/reasoning engine over authoritative open data, not a megaphone. SnoutID(R)/Petnow/NoseID(Iams): nose-print biometric ID that requires pre-registering your pet's snout BEFORE it's lost and the finder using the same app — useless if the finder isn't in that ecosystem; Reunipet needs no pre-enrollment and works off whatever a stranger typed into a public shelter system. In short: incumbents optimize a single biometric inside a single network; Reunipet optimizes coverage + semantic reasoning across many open networks.

pgvector centerpiece: Two embedding columns per pet/intake/sighting record, both indexed with pgvector HNSW. (1) image_embedding (vector(512)) from an open CLIP image encoder run on each photo (lost pet, shelter intake photo, found sighting). (2) text_embedding (vector(1024)) from Bedrock Titan Text Embeddings v2 over a normalized 'pet descriptor string' assembled from species/breed/color/coat/pattern/size/sex/distinctive-marks/name (works even when no photo exists). The semantic query at match time: given a new lost report, do a hybrid candidate retrieval — for each candidate compute (a) cosine distance on image_embedding when both have photos, (b) cosine distance on text_embedding always, then filter/boost by a geo/time corridor (PostGIS ST_DWithin on last-seen vs intake location, scaled by elapsed days) and SQL predicates (species, rough size band, date window). pgvector returns the top-N nearest by a weighted blend of the two distances; those N rows are handed to Claude for final reasoning. Example: lost 'brindle pit-bull, torn left ear' text-embeds and pulls a photoless Travis County intake row reading 'br/wht pit mix, L ear notch, found Zilker' that pure face-match would never surface.

The Aurora data model (what the judges score)

Aurora PostgreSQL with PostGIS + pgvector. CORE RELATIONAL: sources (id, name, type[municipal_socrata|rescuegroups|petfinder|community], license, ingest_url, ts_cols), pets (canonical entity: id, source_id FK, external_ref, species, breed_primary, breed_secondary, color, coat_pattern, size_band, sex, age_band, distinctive_marks JSONB, microchipped bool, name_text, descriptor_string, image_embedding vector(512), text_embedding vector(1024), last_seen_geog GEOGRAPHY(Point), created_at), lost_reports and found_reports (owner/finder side, FK to a pet record, contact, status), matches (lost_id FK, candidate_id FK, image_score, text_score, geo_score, blended_score, claude_verdict TEXT, claude_confidence, state[suggested|viewed|confirmed|rejected], created_at). TIME-SERIES (natural fit): shelter_intake_events as a partitioned/time-series table (event_ts, source_id, animal_ref, intake_type[stray|owner_surrender|found], jurisdiction, geog, raw_payload JSONB) — append-only, declaratively partitioned by month, since municipal feeds are inherently event streams of intakes/outcomes over time; this also powers 'how long until hold-expiry' countdowns. VECTOR: the two embedding columns above with HNSW indexes; GiST index on geography cols; GIN on distinctive_marks JSONB. ANALYTICS / MATERIALIZED VIEWS: mv_reunions_weekly (confirmed matches by week/jurisdiction for the public impact wall and demo), mv_shelter_coverage (count of live ingested rows per source per region — proves the open-data coverage claim visually), mv_active_lost (open lost reports with freshest top candidate, refreshed on new intake), and mv_corridor_stats (median lost-to-found distance/time by species, which actually tunes the geo/time corridor weighting). CHANGE-DATA path: an AFTER INSERT trigger on shelter_intake_events enqueues new rows for embedding + re-match against all active lost_reports, so the 'standing watch' alerts fire automatically. This layered model (relational entities + time-series intake events + dual vectors + PostGIS + materialized analytics) is the centerpiece the database-specialist judges score.

Bedrock Claude agent

A Bedrock Claude 'Reunion Analyst' agent (via Vercel AI SDK) invoked on the top-N pgvector candidates. For each candidate it receives the owner's structured + free-text description and the candidate's raw, messy record (often a terse photoless shelter intake note) and produces: (1) a same-pet verdict with calibrated confidence, reasoning explicitly over which features agree vs conflict and the geo/time plausibility (not just echoing the vector score); (2) a flag for likely data-entry errors in shelter rows (mis-keyed sex/breed is common) so a true match isn't discarded on a bad field; (3) a plain-English, cited action brief — source, kennel/ID, shelter phone, and the computed hold-expiry urgency — with a fixed non-clinical/verify-in-person guardrail in the system prompt. It also normalizes inbound free text into the structured descriptor_string used for embedding (tool-style extraction). Claude is the reasoning/explanation layer on top of retrieval; embeddings are Titan, keeping all runtime AI on Bedrock.

Pawllel
Find the dogs just like yours — and what actually worked for them.
Craft #2
Tech/DB 5 Design 4 Impact 3 Orig. 5 $ Realism 3
Who it's for

Engaged dog owners (especially of rescues, mixed-breeds, and "my dog is weird and I don't know why" cases) who want to understand their dog's personality and quirks beyond breed stereotypes — and want concrete, peer-sourced ideas for what to try. Secondary: prospective adopters comparing a shelter dog's described temperament against the corpus before committing.

The pitch

Breed tells you almost nothing about how your individual dog behaves — peer-reviewed genomics (Darwin's Ark, Science 2022) found breed explains only ~9% of behavioral variation. Pawllel ignores the breed label and instead matches your dog by how it actually behaves. You describe your dog in your own words plus a 2-minute survey; Pawllel embeds that into a "temperament fingerprint," then uses pgvector to find the dogs most behaviorally similar to yours among 18,000+ owner-reported profiles from the open, CC0-licensed Darwin's Ark dataset. The pitch

You log a one-tap mood/comfort check, snap a photo, dictate a quick voice note ("she didn't finish breakfast and seemed wobbly on the back stairs again"), and tick off the day's meds. Who it's for

Owners of one "difficult" adolescent-to-adult dog with a specific, recurring behavior problem — leash reactivity, separation-related distress, resource guarding, or fear/noise sensitivity — who have already failed at generic puppy-trick apps and group obedience classes, can't afford or wait for a $200/session board-certified veterinary behaviorist (VB), and want a structured, science-honest plan with proof it's working. Primary persona: 28-45, urban/suburban, US, spends on the dog, anxious about doing it "wrong." Secondary: force-free trainers (R+) who want a client-facing homework + progress tool between sessions.

The pitch

Most dog apps are content libraries or an "AI trainer" chatbot that gives you the same five tips for every problem. Tideline does the opposite: you describe what your dog actually does, in your own words, and Tideline semantically matches your situation to the specific evidence-based protocol that fits it — Look-At-That and Behavior Adjustment Training for leash reactivity, graduated departure desensitization for separation distress, trade-and-retreat for resource guarding, "treat-retreat" for fear — sourced from veterinary-behavior literature and AVSAB/force-free guidance. Then it does the part nobody else does well: it turns the protocol into a graded "ladder" of micro-sessions and tracks a real progress time-series (trigger distance, latency-to-settle, recovery time, reaction intensity) so you can SEE the comfort line moving outward week over week. A Bedrock Claude coach reads your last 10 logged sessions and tells you exactly whether to advance a rung, hold, or back off — and, critically, flags when your case is out of scope and routes you to a credentialed professional. It's not a chatbot with a dog skin; it's a protocol engine with an honest progress model and a built-in safety brake.

End-to-end user experience
  1. Describe it in your own words
    Onboarding asks one open prompt: 'What does your dog do, and when?' The owner types: 'My terrier mix lunges and barks at other dogs on walks, but only on leash — he's fine at daycare. Starts about 15 feet away.' Plus three structured fields: trigger, context, dog's age/size. No multiple-choice maze.
  2. Semantic match to a protocol (not a generic plan)
    The free-text is embedded and matched (pgvector) against a curated library of behavior-pattern vignettes, each tagged to one evidence-based protocol. Tideline returns: 'This reads as leash/barrier frustration-reactivity, not aggression. Recommended protocol: Engage-Disengage / Look-At-That + distance management.' It shows WHY it matched (nearest reference cases) and cites the AVSAB humane-training statement and the source literature — transparent, not a black box.
  3. Set the baseline (start the time-series)
    Before any training, the app captures a baseline: 'At what distance does he first react?' (15 ft), 'How long to calm down after?' (~90s), intensity 1-5 (4). This writes the first row of the progress time-series and defines rung 0 of the ladder — the dog's current 'comfort line.'
  4. Run a 5-minute guided micro-session
    Tideline serves one concrete rep: 'Stand at 18 ft — just past his line. Mark and feed every time he NOTICES a dog and looks back at you. 8-10 reps, then leave.' A one-tap session logger captures distance worked, # reps, # over-threshold reactions, recovery time, and the owner's confidence. Outcome: a logged, structured datapoint — not a journal entry.
  5. The Claude coach reads the trend and titrates
    After 3-4 logged sessions, the Bedrock Claude coach summarizes the time-series: 'Recovery time dropped 90s -> 35s and you had zero over-threshold reps at 18 ft for two sessions. Advance one rung: try 15 ft.' If reactions spike, it instead says 'Hold — back to 20 ft, you moved too fast,' and explains the threshold concept. This is graded-exposure titration grounded in the owner's own data, with citations on tap.
  6. See the line move (the payoff screen)
    A progress dashboard charts trigger-distance shrinking and latency-to-settle dropping over weeks, with rung milestones annotated ('Week 3: held calm at 12 ft'). A materialized 'streak + trend' view makes improvement legible at a glance — the emotional hook that drives retention and shareability.
  7. Honest safety brake + escalation
    If the owner logs signs Tideline treats as out-of-scope (a bite that breaks skin, freezing/stiffening before lunging suggesting true aggression, or no improvement after a defined window), it stops cheerleading: 'This pattern needs a credentialed professional.' It surfaces the free AVSAB 'How to Choose a Trainer' guidance and a directory link to veterinary behaviorists / CABs. Non-clinical, liability-aware, and it earns trust by knowing its limits.

These are people who currently google "are golden retrievers prone to cancer" and get scattered, fear-mongering blog answers — Pawmanac gives them a grounded, personalized, year-by-year plan instead.

The pitch

Every dog breed carries a known set of health predispositions, and they unfold on a predictable timeline — hip dysplasia screening matters at 18-24 months, certain cancers spike after age 7, brachycephalic airway issues show early. Owners don't learn this until a $4,000 emergency hits. Pawmanac turns decades of veterinary epidemiology (VetCompass breed-disorder prevalence, OMIA genetic disorders, Dog Aging Project comorbidity networks, AAHA life-stage guidelines) into a personalized "health almanac" for YOUR dog: enter breed + age (+ optional DNA mix and weight), and get a forward-looking timeline of the conditions your breed is statistically predisposed to, WHEN each typically presents, the preventive screening that catches it early, and a budgeted cost-prep schedule so the bills don't blindside you. Describe a worry in plain language ("she's been licking her paws raw and scratching") and pgvector semantically matches it against the breed's known condition profile to tell you whether it tracks with a known predisposition (e.g., atopic dermatitis in this breed) and what to ask your vet — always non-diagnostic, always "discuss with your vet." It's foresight and financial preparedness, not a diagnosis and not a shoebox of PDFs.

End-to-end user experience
  1. Pick your dog (or a breed you're considering)
    Landing screen: 'Build your dog's health almanac.' User searches/selects a breed from an autocomplete (powered by The Dog API breed list), enters age (or 'thinking about getting one'), and optionally sex/neuter status and weight. For mixes, they can add up to 3 breed components with rough percentages or paste a DNA-test breed breakdown. One screen, ~20 seconds, no account required to see the first result.
  2. See the Foresight Timeline
    Pawmanac renders a horizontal life timeline (puppy -> young adult -> mature -> senior) seeded from AAHA life stages. Plotted on it are the breed's top predispositions as cards positioned at the age each TYPICALLY becomes relevant — e.g., for a 1-yr-old Golden: 'Hip/elbow screening — recommend by 18-24 mo,' 'Skin/ear atopy — watch ongoing,' 'Cancer surveillance — risk rises after 7.' Each card shows a prevalence figure with its source (VetCompass/OMIA) and a confidence note. This is the 'aha' screen.
  3. Open a condition card -> preventive action + cost band
    Tapping a card expands it: what the condition is in plain English, the breed-specific lifetime risk (cited), the EARLY screening or preventive step that changes the outcome, and an estimated cost band for both prevention (e.g., 'OFA hip screen ~$300') and treatment-if-it-happens (e.g., 'TPLO surgery $3.5-6k'), drawn from published pet-insurance claims ranges. A 'Add to my plan' button schedules the preventive step at the right future age.
  4. Describe a worry in your own words (semantic match)
    A persistent 'Ask Pawmanac' box: user types 'he's been dragging his back end and seems stiff getting up.' Bedrock Claude rewrites this into a clean concern, pgvector semantically searches the breed's condition knowledge base, and Claude returns: 'This pattern overlaps with two conditions your breed is predisposed to — hip dysplasia and degenerative myelopathy. Here's what each looks like, what your vet can check, and the typical age of onset. This is informational, not a diagnosis — please book a vet visit if symptoms persist.' It cites which knowledge cards it matched.
  5. Get your cost-prep schedule
    Pawmanac aggregates the planned preventive steps + risk-weighted potential treatments into a 'Cost Almanac': a year-by-year projected spend (routine + screening) and a suggested monthly set-aside (e.g., '$48/mo into a pet fund covers your breed's expected preventive care + a cushion for the 2 most likely big-ticket events'). It contrasts self-funding vs. insurance break-even using public claims-cost ranges — grounded, not salesy.
  6. Save the almanac + opt into seasonal nudges
    User creates a free account to save the dog. Pawmanac becomes a living almanac: as the dog ages, cards re-prioritize and the app sends a few well-timed nudges ('Bella turns 7 next month — this is when senior bloodwork and cancer surveillance start paying off for Goldens'). Materialized analytics views drive these age-triggered prompts.
  7. Export a vet-visit brief
    Before a checkup, user taps 'Prep for vet': Pawmanac generates a one-page brief — breed predispositions due to discuss at this age, the owner's logged concerns, and 4-5 specific questions to ask. Frames Pawmanac as the owner's prep tool that makes the vet visit better, NOT a replacement for it (key liability posture).

pgvector centerpiece: EMBEDDED: (a) a curated knowledge base of breed-condition 'cards' — each a short canonical description of a disorder as it presents in a given breed (clinical signs in plain language, typical age of onset, preventive/screening action, prevalence, source), embedded via Bedrock Titan Text Embeddings v2; (b) condition definitions and synonym/lay-term expansions (so 'scooting,' 'dragging back end,' 'paw licking' map to clinical concepts); (c) optionally, abstracts/snippets from the cited open-access VetCompass/OMIA literature for 'why we say this.' SEMANTIC QUERY: when an owner describes a worry in free text, Bedrock Claude normalizes it, we embed it, then run a pgvector cosine-similarity search (HNSW index, vector_cosine_ops) FILTERED to the candidate condition set for that dog's breed mix (a hybrid query: WHERE condition_id IN (breed's predisposition set) ORDER BY embedding <=> :query LIMIT k). This returns the most semantically relevant KNOWN predispositions, which Claude then explains and grounds. Same vector layer powers 'find similar conditions' and dedupes overlapping disorders across breeds in a mix.

The Aurora data model (what the judges score)

Aurora PostgreSQL (Serverless v2) with pgvector. RELATIONAL CORE (3NF): breeds(breed_id, name, size_group, brachycephalic_flag, akc_group); conditions(condition_id, name, body_system, omia_ref, is_genetic, severity_tier); breed_condition_risk(breed_id, condition_id, lifetime_prevalence, relative_risk, typical_onset_age_months_low/high, evidence_source, confidence) — the heart of the model, a many-to-many risk graph; condition_action(condition_id, life_stage, action_type[screen|preventive|monitor], description, cost_band_low/high, cost_source); life_stages(stage, age_months_low/high) seeded from AAHA; cost_ranges(condition_id, cost_type[prevention|treatment], low, high, currency, source). USER SIDE: users, dogs(dog_id, user_id, primary_breed_id, sex, neuter_status, weight_kg, birthdate), dog_breed_mix(dog_id, breed_id, fraction) for mixes/DNA imports. TIME-SERIES (natural fit): care_events(dog_id, event_ts, event_type, condition_id, cost, notes) and weight_log(dog_id, ts, weight_kg) — append-only, time-ordered; partition care_events by RANGE on event_ts (monthly) for clean time-series queries and retention. VECTOR: condition_embeddings(condition_id, breed_id NULLABLE, embedding vector(1024), text_chunk) with an HNSW index (vector_cosine_ops). ANALYTICS / MATERIALIZED VIEWS — the showpiece for database judges: (1) mv_dog_timeline — joins a dog's breed mix to breed_condition_risk and life_stages to precompute each dog's age-positioned risk cards (refreshed on dog edit); (2) mv_breed_cost_curve — per-breed projected annual preventive + risk-weighted treatment spend by age, feeding the cost almanac; (3) mv_age_trigger_queue — dogs crossing an onset threshold in the next 30 days, driving seasonal nudges (a cheap cron over a materialized view instead of per-row scans); (4) a comorbidity-edge view derived from Dog Aging Project networks so 'dogs with condition A often also face B' is a precomputed graph edge. Indexing: btree on (breed_id, condition_id), partial indexes on active dogs, GIN on lay-term synonym arrays for hybrid lexical+vector retrieval. This combination — normalized risk graph + partitioned time-series + vector index + four purpose-built materialized views — is what makes the data model 'impressive' rather than a CRUD schema.

Bedrock Claude agent

Bedrock Claude (Sonnet via the Vercel AI SDK Bedrock provider) is the grounding + reasoning layer, deliberately constrained to stay non-clinical. Three jobs: (1) CONCERN NORMALIZER + RETRIEVER (agentic tool use): given a free-text worry, Claude calls a 'search_breed_conditions' tool that runs the pgvector hybrid query, gets back the top-k matched condition cards (with sources), and produces a grounded, citation-bearing explanation that always ends in a 'see your vet' referral and NEVER asserts a diagnosis. It is instructed to refuse/deflect emergency-sounding inputs to 'contact a vet/ER now.' (2) TIMELINE NARRATOR: turns the structured mv_dog_timeline rows into readable, reassuring-but-honest card copy ('here's what's worth watching for your breed at this age, and why'). (3) COST-PREP EXPLAINER: narrates the mv_breed_cost_curve numbers into the self-fund-vs-insurance break-even. Guardrails: retrieval-augmented only (Claude may only discuss conditions returned from the DB, reducing hallucination), structured outputs validated against the condition set, a hard system-prompt boundary that it is an educational prep tool not a diagnostic service. This RAG-over-a-curated-DB design is also what keeps it defensible on liability.
